## Preamble

This Constitution governs collaboration between human authors and artificial-intelligence (AI) systems. It proceeds from a single conviction: that responsibility for intellectual work is non-delegable, and that the worth of such work derives from the human understanding and judgment invested in it.

This conviction is older than computing. The idea that consequential judgment must be reserved to accountable persons descends from the charter tradition (Magna Carta, 1215), and the commitment to the dignity and worth of the human person is affirmed at the foundation of the modern international order (Charter of the United Nations, 1945). The same idea is now restated for AI specifically: that AI systems must remain under meaningful human oversight (European Parliament & Council, 2024; European Commission HLEG, 2019; OECD, 2024), and that the purpose of AI is to augment human intelligence, not to replace it (IBM, 2018). This Constitution adopts that principle as the condition under which the use of AI remains honest, rigorous, and educative.

## Articles

### Article 1 — Human Responsibility

Artificial intelligence is a tool that supports human thinking; it does not replace it. Every submitted work must be conceived, directed, critically reviewed, and approved by a human author who understands it.

### Article 2 — Accountability

The human author bears full and undivided responsibility for the accuracy, rigor, originality, ethics, and quality of any report, paper, analysis, or software artifact. Responsibility cannot be transferred to an AI system, which can neither comprehend the work nor answer for it.

### Article 3 — Verification Before Submission

No work shall be submitted without thorough human review. Facts, references, calculations, source code, interpretations, and conclusions must be independently verified where their correctness matters. Because human vigilance over reliable automation tends to degrade over time, verification shall rest on structural safeguards — independent re-derivation of critical results, adversarial review, and sampling — and not on attention alone.

### Article 4 — Transparency

AI assistance shall be used honestly and disclosed in accordance with the policies of the institution, publisher, employer, or community receiving the work. Disclosure identifies that AI was used, which systems were used, and how.

### Article 5 — Critical Thinking First

AI-generated outputs are suggestions, not truths. Human judgment, domain expertise, and evidence-based reasoning take precedence over automated recommendations. The author shall remain alert to automation bias — the tendency to over-rely on plausible automated output — and shall treat an AI assertion as a prompt for inquiry rather than as evidence in itself.

### Article 6 — Beneficial Use

AI shall be used to enhance learning, creativity, productivity, and scientific inquiry while respecting academic integrity, human dignity, fairness and non-discrimination, and the pursuit of knowledge.

### Article 7 — Continuous Learning

The goal is not merely to produce outputs, but to develop understanding, competence, and independent thinking. AI should help humans learn, not substitute for learning. The use of AI must not erode the capabilities it is meant to support.

---

## Final Principle

> AI may assist in the creation of work; only humans may take responsibility for it.

## Sources and Inspirations

A short, deliberately non-exhaustive list of the instruments that inform this Constitution, with a note on what each connects to. These instruments are referenced by name; their text is not reproduced here.

- **Magna Carta (1215), cl. 39.** The early statement that consequential judgment is reserved to lawful, accountable process rather than exercised arbitrarily. *Inspiration for the charter form and for Articles 1–2.*
- **Charter of the United Nations (1945), Preamble.** Affirms faith in "the dignity and worth of the human person." *Underlies the human-dignity commitment in Article 6.*
- **European Commission, High-Level Expert Group on AI. (2019). _Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI_.** Lists "human agency and oversight" first among its requirements for trustworthy AI. *Underlies Articles 1, 3, and 5.*
- **European Parliament & Council. (2024). _Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (Artificial Intelligence Act)_, Art. 14.** Requires effective human oversight of high-risk AI, and that overseers remain aware of automation bias. *Underlies Articles 1, 3, and 5.*
- **OECD. (2024). _Recommendation of the Council on Artificial Intelligence_ (OECD/LEGAL/0449; orig. 2019, rev. 2024).** The first intergovernmental AI standard; emphasizes transparency, accountability, and human-centred values. *Underlies Articles 2, 4, and 6.*
- **IBM. (2018). _Principles for Trust and Transparency_; IBM Think, "AI ethics" and "AI governance."** States that the purpose of AI is to augment human intelligence, not replace it, and frames accountability for decision-makers. *Accessible reading for managers; underlies Articles 1, 2, and 4.*
